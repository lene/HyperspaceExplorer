Basics about displaying objects and projection

The way to visualizing four- and even higherdimensional objects is, while intellectually demanding (to say the least), mathematically straightforward. Just go ahead and project objects from 4-space to the familiar 3-space, as you project objects from 3-space to a 2-plane. How you go about projecting objects from three to two dimensions, has been known for centuries.

[here goes a picture]

All you need is an eye ('camera' in computer-graphics language), an object you want to visualize and an image plane to project the vertices defining the object onto, one by one. You can calculate the screen coordinates as

xscr = xobj * (zscr - zcam) / (zobj - zcam)

yscr = yobj * (zscr - zcam) / (zobj - zcam)

This projection method extends readily to the projection from four-dimensional to three-dimensional space:

[no picture here, well...]

xscr = xobj * (wscr - wcam) / (wobj - wcam)

yscr = yobj * (wscr - wcam) / (wobj - wcam)

zscr = zobj * (wscr - wcam) / (wobj - wcam)

Of course, to view the three-dimensional projection of a four-dimensional object on a computer monitor (or a piece of paper, or whatever means of viewing pictures is available to you), you must project it down another dimension, onto a two-dimensional plane.

While this part is reasonably straightforward, there are some pitfalls.

One issue is that we have viewed the camera as stationary to keep the math simple. If you want to see your object from different angles, there are two possibilities: move the camera around the object, or rotate the object. In my work, i have taken the latter approach. This way a counterintuitive phenomenon appears: in 4-space, rotations do not take place around axes. They take place around planes.

The other pitfall i want to mention: do not confuse the 4D and the 3D viewpoint. The former defines your projection from four- to three-space, the latter the projection from three- to two-space. They are totally independent. You can project an object from 4D to 3D, and then change the 3D viewpoint as you wish, without having to change the 4D viewpoint.

Menger Sponge


One of my favourites... a three-dimensional Menger Sponge is constructed this way:

Take a cube.

Subdivide each face of the cube in 9 squares. You'll slice the cube in 27 smaller cubes.

Remove the cube in the middle of every face, as well as the centermost cube.

This is what i call a 'level 1' Menger Sponge. To construct a level n sponge, assemble 20 level n-1 sponges in the above way.

A four-dimensional sponge is created similarly:

Take a hypercube.

Subdivide each surspace of the cube in 27 cubes. You'll slice the hypercube in 81 smaller hypercubes.

Remove the hypercube in the middle of every surspace, as well as the centermost hypercube.

Regular 4-Simplex

Lacking a better name for the four-dimensional analog of a regular tetrahedron, I call it "Regular 4-simplex". You can construct a 4-simplex fron a tetrahedron (or 3-simplex) by defining a point on the w-axis (or any point that does not lie in a 3-space with the tetrahedron) and connecting it to all four vertices of the tetrahedron.

Any four-dimensional object can be approximated as a ... of 4-simplices, just as any surface can be approximated by triangles and every body by tetrahedra.
